# Intro

Over the course of my Spanish learning journey, I watched a lot of content on the history of Spain and developed a fascination with the Basque region. The distinct cultural identity sitting between France and Spain, and the Basque language isolate was really intriguing to me. When it came time to plan my first-ever trip to Spain for my dad's 65th birthday, I knew that Basque Country was the region I wanted to visit most.

After a few days in Madrid, we took the train direct to Bilbao. The train was about five hours and left the station at 7:20 AM. We left our hotel around 6 AM to walk to the train station, and my dad really got a kick out of all the partygoers stumbling out of the clubs as they began to close. The train ride itself was uneventful as I was drifting in and out of sleep, but the landscapes around Valladolid were stunning. The plan was to spend two nights in Bilbao and three nights in San Sebastian.

I booked a "serviced apartment" at Líbere Bilbao Ledesma for our two nights in Bilbao. This seemed like a decent middle ground between a hotel and Airbnb, but by the end of our stay I was wishing that I had just booked a hotel. The AC didn't work, they had run out of welcome boxes with laundry detergent and other supplies (the washing machine being the main reason I opted for an apartment) and the process to store our luggage was more convoluted than necessary. I think I'm just swearing off Airbnbs and serviced apartments after this.

The place we booked in San Sebastian was some sort of hostel and hotel hybrid, and I stayed in a Goldilocks-style bedroom with three twin beds equally spaced apart. I must have been too used to sleeping on sofa beds by this point, because I accidentally fell off the mattress in the middle of the night, leading my parents to believe that a bomb had gone off. The base of the bed platform was light and unstable, but this had never happened to me before. Maybe too much cider at dinner?

# Basque Cuisine

I was most excited for the food (no surprise), which is a mix of fresh seafood, grilled meats and local produce - hearty country food through and through.

Here are some of my favorite food and drinks, although I didn't get photos of some 😭:

## Pintxos

I had booked a pintxos walking tour for our first night in Bilbao. The guide explained the difference between tapas and pintxos (Pintxos are single-serve, small snacks with a skewer and typically served on bread. I do like having the idea of getting to hoard a pintxo all to myself...)

- Gratinado de txangurro at [El Globo](https://maps.app.goo.gl/1Z1HVvXevEef7dSD8)
  - Creamy, gratinated golden brown spider crab on top of a delightfully crispy toast. This was DELICIOUS.
- Shrimp pintxo at [Bar Ledesma](https://maps.app.goo.gl/Mb9PUMYFH9dS2NAJ8)
  - This bar was across the street from our place in Bilbao. To be honest, I don't even know if this pintxo has a name, I just pointed at it and ordered. The caviar didn't do much for me, but the texture with the creamy shrimp salad (?) was great.
    ![[Attachments/CDAC303B-6A9C-489B-AE12-64C71979C7D6_1_105_c.jpeg|center|363]]
- Anchovy/green pepper at [Maritxu Taberna](https://maps.app.goo.gl/aDyEKyxTR2125gzw7)
  - Again, not sure if this had a name. It only consisted of an anchovy filet and a slice of grilled green pepper on a mini toast, but it was simply fantastic. Something to recreate at home, for sure.
  - Maritxu Taberna was extremely unassuming, and I originally popped in because I wanted the menú del día for a quick, budget friendly lunch. My lunch came with a drink, salad, soup, bread and entree for around 15 euros. Everything was tasty and made me agree with the sentiment that you can walk into any restaurant in San Sebastian and find something delicious. I usually disagree with the sense of exceptionalism around the food in Europe and how everything tastes sooo much better than in the US, etc, etc...but I'd be hard pressed to find a simple meal this good and cheap in San Diego.

## Txuletas

Dry-aged, bone-in ribeye from mature cows that produces an insanely tender and beefy flavor. I had three over the course of my five days in Basque Country. I rarely eat beef, and usually no more than a few bites - but all three were incredible, and I'd do it all over again.

- [Asador Guetaria](https://maps.app.goo.gl/a46abgkGwxRzKWrk8)
  - The first restaurant out of six(!) that was willing to seat us for dinner on Sunday night in Bilbao. A traditional steakhouse with strangely ornate decor, this txuleta was served with fries and grilled peppers. It's also the most well-done txuleta that I ate, and I would already consider this medium rare.
    ![[Attachments/D4334FF2-0900-4C47-B213-D5E04DAF215A_1_102_o.jpeg|center|363]]

- [Lizeaga](https://maps.app.goo.gl/Ljo5hV6KPB7gdqaYA)
  - Txuleta is a traditional dish served at Basque cider houses. A quick search on Reddit for San Sebastian recommendations led me to Lizeaga, where I was drawn by the prospect of unlimited cider from the barrel (from January to April only, and for the rest of the months cider is served from the bottle. This was the end of April, so my cider barrel dreams came true.) We went for dinner during the first night in San Sebastian. Lizeaga was located around 3 miles from our hotel, and it was the only time I had to call an Uber during our entire Spain trip.
  - Everything is served family style, and eaten standing around a table. They brought out a decent-sized txuleta for the three of us and everyone agreed that this steak was better than the night before in Bilbao. Our host overheard my dad saying that he could go for another one, and she jumped in to tell us that each txuleta was "only" meant for 2 people and she was happy to toss another one on the grill for us. We finished txuleta #2 but my family and I promptly fell into a food coma after. Still worth it!

- [Bodega Donostiarra](https://maps.app.goo.gl/jP4bY3c6n5qUcFYf6)
  - I snagged a table at Bodega Donostiarra for our last night in San Sebastian. They run a tight ship here, and give you 90 minutes to eat your meal before turning it over to the next party. It seems a little antithetical to the concept of sobremesa, but the txuleta was great here as well, so all is forgiven.

## Desserts

I must add a tearful note here and say I didn't get a chance to have any Basque cheesecake while actually in Basque Country. Whenever I'm back in San Sebastian, I'd love to try the cheesecake from [La Viña](https://maps.app.goo.gl/maZ1U2JVCRofwAbm6). I'm still kicking myself for it, but there was no lack of sweet treats either.

- Bollo de Mantequilla at [Arrese](https://maps.app.goo.gl/KitNi4uuGXhZn3zNA)
  - I wanted to try this butter bun after watching this [El Comidista video](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=tuL5J6kduIo) reviewing Bilbao's bollos de mantequilla. Pasteleria Arrese is a chain that didn't receive high marks from the Comidista himself, but it was still recommended by the guide from the pintxos tour. It reminded me of a butter cream bread from Paris Baguette. I ordered one to go and ate it at the park nearby.
    ![[Attachments/AB21E583-6709-41E8-83BF-B7C93B01EED5_1_105_c.jpeg|center|363]]
- Bollo de Mantequilla #2 at [Labeko Okindegia](https://maps.app.goo.gl/m9DTvu7YwDmC3x3x5)
  - One of the highest rated buns from the video, and this is the one that made me see the vision. Fluffy, light bread and a generous spread of sweetened butter. I like this oblong shape better, too. Once again eaten at the park.
    ![[Attachments/F2C12869-06C6-4E1C-8476-C66253CA45D5_1_105_c.jpeg|center|363]]
- Sheep Cheese and Quince Jelly, Walnuts, Biscuits at Lizeaga
  - This was the simple dessert round served at Lizeaga after taking down two txuletas. Everyone was pretty full by this point, but my dad started cracking away at the walnuts on the table. He loves an interactive part of the meal! Nibbling on the biscuits and jelly was a nice way to end the meal. My mom, ever the conservationist, decided to wrap the cheese and take it back to our hotel. Yes, the fridge still smelled like cheese by the time we checked out.
    ![[Attachments/4CD21119-AF6B-4649-B64A-B01C67864A96_1_105_c.jpeg|center|363]]

## Other Favorites

- Basque Cider
  - A tart, cloudy cider that's traditionally poured from a distance (around 3 feet) to help with aeration. The taste reminded me of kombucha, and definitely not like any cider I've had in the US. Drinking cider from the barrel was really interactive. The brewers unplug a peg from the barrel which starts the cider flow, and everybody lines up to pour themselves a glass while (ideally) not letting any cider fall to the ground.
- White Asparagus with Sesame and Hazelnut Puree at [Narru](https://maps.app.goo.gl/bWKC8yWv6LQUfDLP6)
  My first time having white asparagus. Such a tender, silky texture complemented by the hazelnut cream sauce. Something to recreate once I can get my hands on white asparagus locally.
  ![[Attachments/10ED1C4A-42D9-492D-A457-340EBF9A3839_1_105_c.jpeg|center|363]]
- Carajillo at [Pokhara](https://maps.app.goo.gl/EbsbaYetN4cixt3TA)
  - My parents went off to the beach so my mom could go surfing, so I had time to wander around. After stumbling around and checking out the library at [Tabakalera](https://maps.app.goo.gl/Lbte78PtfZoGzZ8Z9), I popped into Pokhara for a carajillo. My mom had ordered one at Narru the night before, and we were both disappointed. I ordered my carajillo with rum, and it came with this cinnamon sweet foam on top. Sweet foam isn't usually my thing, but this one really brought out the rum. Impressive soundsystem here too, the bartenders had a great New Wave playlist on while I was there. I made some decent progress with [_Dead Lions_](https://www.goodreads.com/en/book/show/61677576-dead-lions) while I was there, and I left with a delightful buzz.

## So much left to see...

Basque Country left such an impression on me, and I can't wait to return. I spent some time at the [San Telmo Museum](https://maps.app.goo.gl/Bsg48wh87gU9sXiV9) and scratched the surface of how the climate, proximity to the coast and geographical landscape have shaped the region. When popping into a San Sebastian bookstore, the most comprehensive English-language book on Basque history that I found was Mark Kurlansky's [_The Basque History of the World_](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/350206), published in 1999. I don't know enough about the turbulent, violent era of the 20th century and how the separatist movement is viewed today. (Just an insight: my tour guide did mention that she identifies as a Basque first and would prefer that Basque Country is independent from Spain, if only there were EU support.) All in all, it was such a fascinating place that didn't feel like Spain or France as I understood them. I wouldn't consider it a budget-friendly place, but it's so warm and culturally rich that I'll be back, especially to try that cheesecake.
