Recipe from Nothing Fancy by Alison Roman
![[Attachments/Pasted image 20260622144522.png|center|363]]

This Father's Day, I decided to return to a recipe that I had made when my parents visited me in Austin for Thanksgiving 2023. My parents and I aren't big on turkey for Thanksgiving, especially when it's just us three. I had made braised short ribs when they visited the year prior, so a rib roast seemed like a logical evolution to a fancy yet non-fussy holiday meal. The meal turned out phenomenally, it honestly seems much more intimidating than it really is - but I hadn't touched a rib roast for nearly three years since. Being back in San Diego for Father's Day seemed like the perfect excuse to attempt this again.

I remember buying the original rib roast for about \$30/lb at Central Market, but when I went to buy the rib roast at Seaside Market this past week, they were being sold for \$25.99/lb. This pricing seems standard to me? I understand that I can probably get some more artisanal beef in the \$35 - 40 range at a specialty butcher shop, but this was the highest quality meat that was accessible to me.

The [recipe](https://www.alisoneroman.com/recipes/low-and-slow-rib-roast-with-anchovy-and-rosemary/) I use calls for a 3-bone roast (about 6 - 7½ lbs), and when I asked for the cut at the meat counter, the butcher told me it would be closer to 8 - 10 lbs. I decided to just do the 2-bone roast, but when the butcher cut the roast it ended up being 3 bones and also the perfect weight?? I'm not sure if this was a miscommunication, but I'm not complaining:

![[Attachments/2B405819-2F9F-4159-82F3-064E74959631_1_105_c.jpeg|center|363]]Just like last time, I salted the meat and let it rest for 48 hours in the fridge. I reallly believe that the 48 hour cure makes a huge difference and would always plan for this in the future. For the rosemary/garlic/anchovy marinade, I used about 15 anchovies. I probably could have used more of all the marinade ingredients - my marinade ended up being pretty loose after adding the olive oil, it didn't really resemble a paste that spread evenly. I didn't have enough of the marinade to spread on the sides of the roast, just the top section. Ultimately this didn't make a difference, but adding more marinade is probably better if the roast size is near the top of the range.

The part that I (naively) didn't account for was the difference in oven temps between the oven at my parent's place and my old Austin apartment. My 2023 roast took around 3½ hours at 250F and was on the rare side. Aiming for a medium rare this time, I checked the rib roast temp at the 2 hour, 2½ hour and 3 hour mark. Alison recommends to take the oven out at an internal temp of 110F, which will eventually rise to the targeted 125F for a medium-rare cut. Checking my rib roast temp at the 3 hour mark yielded an internal temp of 128 or so, and I was initially worried that I blew a \$200 rib roast.

The roast ended up being a true medium, which I wasn't complaining about. (I was also reassured by this [Serious Eats recipe](https://www.seriouseats.com/perfect-prime-rib-beef-recipe) where Kenji Lopez-Alt prefers a reverse seared prime rib cooked to medium...) I skipped the step to brown the roast on the stovetop, since the marinade formed a really nice golden brown crust that seemed perfect already. The more well-done slices at the edges were still incredibly tender, and the anchovy from the marinade adds this incredible umami funk that complements the steak really well. It's not fishy at all!

I would say that dinner still turned out great, and my parents kept reassuring me that the prime rib was fantastic. The reverse sear method yielded phenomenal results. I haven't made a rib roast any other way, but the reverse sear is super hands off. I love that the long cook time and ability to let the steak rest for hours means that you're free to prep other dishes or put out other holiday-related fires. This is a recipe that I'll return to time and time again.
