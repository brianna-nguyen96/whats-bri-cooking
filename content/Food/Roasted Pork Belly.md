Recipe from Vietnamese by Uyen Luu

![[Attachments/4DFD1B44-F6C2-4A38-BD61-B62CC0690002_1_105_c.jpeg|center|363]]

As embarrassing as it is, I made this recipe because I was inspired by the cooking videos/vlogs of Diegodoal, a food content creator based in Spain. Having a daily goal of 90 minutes for Spanish learning means that short, concise cooking videos take up a lot of my watchtime. He went to Malaysia and encountered a roasted pork belly dish served with rice, and recreated it [here](https://youtu.be/ZXEHyaJWQ88?si=Ums5ok7z4wNLK8zK).

My family most often eats roasted pork belly (_heo quay_) in bánh hỏi, but we usually buy it from Sieu Sieu BBQ, which my aunt insists has the best pork belly in all of San Diego. Last time we bought pork belly from Sieu Sieu, I remember paying around $18/lb. Pork belly is sold at the 99 Ranch meat counter for like, $7/lb - which seemed like a steal, especially because everything I saw pointed to roasted pork belly being soooo easy to make.

I used the recipe from the cookbook Vietnamese, since it's one of my favorite cookbooks and usually produces great results. You're supposed to score the pork belly into 1cm squares, and you can see exactly how successful I was. I couldn't get a good hold on exactly how much pressure to exert in order to score the skin and ended up cutting into the pork belly by about 3 inches in some sections, lol.

The recipe begins by pouring boiling water over the pork to remove excess fat and impurities. Not sure if this actually made a difference, but sure. The pork is marinated in a mix of garlic powder, sea salt, soy sauce and five-spice powder. (Note: I think I subbed a mix of cumin/cinnamon? I know for sure that I don't have five-spice at home.)

Uyen's recipe calls for leaving the marinade on for 2 hours before popping the pork in the oven, but Diego lets the pork belly chill in the fridge overnight. I went with waiting overnight, because I assumed it would lead to better results - again, not sure if this made a difference re: skin crispiness.

The pork belly then goes into the oven at 425 F for about an hour. After letting it cool, you cut up the pork into bite-size pieces and serve with the sauce, noodles, lettuce and herbs.

![[Attachments/28A4F1EE-BAF4-40C0-9778-03AF55AD2B4B_1_105_c.jpeg|center|363]]

Overall, the pork belly was good, although I didn't exactly get that bubbly, shatter-y texture that Sieu Sieu has - this pork belly was still crunchy but without a real satisfying crisp. This still tasted great as part of bánh hỏi, and the original 2 lbs that we bought was gone pretty quickly. I wouldn't exactly be ready to serve the pork belly to my friends, but I'd be happy to try this recipe again.
