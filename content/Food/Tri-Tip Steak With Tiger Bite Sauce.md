Recipe from Bon Appetit by Yia Vang

![[A86000FF-6A3E-4E51-B72B-3DCBC35BF296_1_105_c 1.jpeg|center|363]]

Hello! I’ve been toying with the idea of creating my own recipe log. I’ve also missed a creative and curated outlet of my own, so I’m actually really excited to get to write a little more and expand on my process. I don’t expect too much (and you definitely shouldn’t either!) but life has really thrown me a curveball lately, and now I find myself with a lot of time, inspiration and a much larger kitchen than in my previous Austin apartment.

Anyways - this recipe was on the cover of Bon Appetit back in 2020, but I didn’t come across it until I was searching for tri-tip recipes a week ago. Vons had tri-tip on sale for $4.99/lb, and Chris and I had never cooked tri-tip before. I most commonly associate tri-tip either with Cardiff crack (s/o Seaside Market), or eaten alongside with chimichurri. The accompanying “tiger bite sauce” is described as super umami, made with crushed & charred cherry tomatoes, oyster sauce, fish sauce, lime juice and a paste of cilantro/shallot/garlic/Thai chilies. 

![[03DDB904-8B66-4D13-8079-FEB48D544968_1_105_c.jpeg|center||363]]

I ended up buying a 3ish lb roast of tri-tip. The dry rub consisted of a blend of Sichuan peppercorns, coriander seeds and cumin seeds. I was a little grouchy at having to pay for an entire jar of cumin and coriander seeds at Vons, I wish every supermarket had a bulk spice section. (I could easily write another 1000 words here about how much I miss Central Market and their bulk section. #1 grocery store in the world!!!!!) 

![[56664ABB-C58E-4A7A-B286-C7BED4D63E49_1_105_c.jpeg|center||363]]

The original recipe calls for a 2 lb tri-tip steak but I kept the ratio of meat to dry rub the same, which ended up working perfectly. I’m Sichuan peppercorn-agnostic I suppose, I think it’s easy to overdo it and I never really understood how or why it’s become sort of buzzy? It seems like a logical evolution of trendy spices in a post-Chili crisp world, but I don’t know. 

I marinated the steak for about 4 hours in the fridge, which is actually the minimum marinade time in the recipe. My parents’ place doesn’t have a grill, so Chris went for the reverse sear method in the oven for an hour of cooking time. After quickly finishing it on the grill, I made the cilantro paste and added it to the tomato mixture along with the fish and oyster sauce. (I’ve been using my food processor much more lately, actually. I came home to my stash of cookbooks that I never brought to Austin and began to tackle the Night & Market cookbook which contains subrecipes within subrecipes, most of which seem to require a food processor. This is probably why I never brought it to Austin, lol.)

The recipe mentions serving the tri-tip alongside some sticky rice, but I was waaaay too lazy to cook rice. There was some leftover romaine in the fridge, so I just ate it with that in a steak salad. This is where I must confess that I didn’t bother with food styling or even food photography AT ALL, so we can only go up from here, as far as food blogging goes. Chris and I thought the tri-tip was pretty good, there was a pleasant tingly sensation from the peppercorn but it wasn’t overdone. If anything, I might tone down the cumin next time because the current flavors of spicy + earthy + tingly had a lot going on. The accompanying sauce was tangy, spicy and funky. VERY Southeast Asian in my opinion, which I obviously love but could be offputting for some. I’ve had steak cooked with a splash of fish sauce before, but never had a steak sauce with fish and oyster sauce as a primary component. Even my parents were initially skeptical about it, but ended up liking the dish even more than Chris and I did. We ended up finishing the entire roast, which is a slight bummer as I was looking forward to having it in a steak sandwich the day after… 

Chris absolutely killed it on cooking the steak, the reverse sear was perfect considering the limitations of having no grill. The meat itself was lean but still tender, and surprisingly juicy? I don’t eat beef regularly, so I’m glad that the tri-tip turned out so well for a special occasion meal. I’m not rushing to cook another roast, but glad to have completed this one for some variety with Western style steaks.
